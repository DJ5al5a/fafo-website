Court-Appointed Attorneys in Dependency Cases

You Have the Right to an Attorney

If your children are removed by DCF, you have a constitutional right to an attorney in dependency court.

  • The court will appoint an attorney if you cannot afford one
  • There is a $50 application fee for indigency determination
  • Apply at your first court hearing (shelter hearing)
  • Your attorney stays with you throughout the entire case

Tell the judge at your first hearing that you need an attorney.

Legal Aid Eligibility

General Guidelines

Most legal aid organizations require household income at or below 125-200% of federal poverty level. However:

  • Veterans and seniors may have different criteria
  • Domestic violence victims often receive priority
  • Emergency situations may be handled differently
  • Each organization sets its own eligibility requirements

Call the specific organization to check if you qualify. Don't assume you don't qualify - ask!

ADA Accommodations & Disability Services

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), persons with disabilities are entitled to accommodations to participate fully in court proceedings at no cost.

Available Accommodations

  • Sign Language Interpreters - American Sign Language interpreters for deaf or hard of hearing participants
  • Spoken Language Interpreters - Qualified interpreters provided in dependency cases where needed
  • Assistive Listening Devices - For participants with hearing impairments
  • Accessible Courtrooms - Wheelchair accessible courtroom assignments
  • Large Print Documents - Court documents in accessible formats
  • Other Auxiliary Aids - Additional services as needed for communication disabilities

How to Request Accommodations

Request as far in advance as possible, preferably at least 7 days before your court date.

  • Requests can be made in writing, on a form, or orally
  • Contact the ADA Coordinator for your judicial circuit
  • There is no cost to you for accommodations
  • All accommodations are confidential

Important

Don't skip court because you need accommodations. Contact the court immediately if you need help. Missing court hearings can result in losing your case, even if you have a legitimate disability-related reason.

Guardian ad Litem (GAL) Program

What GAL Does:

  • Court appoints GAL to represent your child's best interests (not yours)
  • Team includes volunteer advocate, attorney, and case manager
  • Visits child regularly and reports to court
  • Stays with case until permanency achieved
  • No cost to parents - automatically appointed in dependency cases

Important to Understand

The Guardian ad Litem represents the child, not you. They work for what they believe is the child's best interest. You may agree or disagree with their recommendations. GAL is not your advocate - your own attorney is.

DCF Oversight & Watchdog Organizations

These official government agencies monitor DCF's compliance with state and federal law, investigate complaints, and provide accountability. If DCF violates policy or law, these agencies may have authority to investigate or audit their actions.

OPPAGA - Legislative Watchdog

Office of Program Policy Analysis and Government Accountability

Research arm of the Florida Legislature that conducts oversight, audits, and evaluative research on DCF and other state agencies. OPPAGA monitors DCF compliance with state law and reports to the Legislature.

What they do: Program reviews, performance audits, policy analysis, and annual reports on DCF programs.

DCF Office of Inspector General

Internal watchdog agency within DCF that conducts investigations and audits of DCF operations.

What they do: Investigate fraud, waste, abuse, and misconduct within DCF. Conduct internal audits and compliance reviews.

Federal Administration for Children & Families (ACF)

Federal agency that oversees state child welfare agencies and ensures compliance with federal law.

What they do: Child and Family Services Reviews (CFSRs), monitor Title IV-E funding compliance, investigate systemic issues.

Contact: Report violations of federal child welfare law to ACF Region 4 (covers Florida).

Visit ACF Website →

How to Use Oversight Agencies

  • Document everything - Oversight agencies need detailed evidence of violations
  • File formal complaints - Written complaints carry more weight than verbal reports
  • Cite specific laws or policies - Reference Florida Statutes, DCF policy (CFOP), or federal regulations that were violated
  • Follow up - Track your complaint and request status updates
  • Be patient but persistent - Investigations take time, but oversight agencies have authority DCF must respect

Important Limitations

Oversight agencies typically review systemic issues and policy compliance, not individual case decisions. They cannot override a court order or make case-specific determinations. However, evidence of widespread violations or patterns of misconduct can result in audits, investigations, and policy changes.
