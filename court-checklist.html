Court Preparation Checklist

Everything you need to bring, know, and do for dependency court hearings

🚨 Before You Go to Court

  • Arrive 30 minutes early - Court starts on time, and being late looks bad
  • Bring your attorney's contact info - In case you need to reach them
  • Turn off your phone - Not on vibrate. OFF. Judges will hold you in contempt
  • No children in court - Unless specifically ordered to bring them
  • Be respectful to everyone - Judges, bailiffs, opposing counsel, DCF workers - everyone

📋 What to Bring to Court

✅ Required Documents (Bring Copies!)

  • Photo ID (driver's license, state ID, passport)
  • Court notice/summons with date, time, location
  • Case number (written down or memorized)
  • All court orders received so far
  • Your case plan (current version)
  • Attorney's business card or contact info

📁 Evidence of Progress

  • Service completion certificates (parenting class, counseling, etc.)
  • Attendance records from all required services
  • Clean drug test results (bring all results, not just recent)
  • Employment verification (pay stubs, offer letter)
  • Housing verification (lease, utility bills in your name)
  • Character reference letters (from employers, clergy, etc.)

📝 Documentation Binder

  • Visit logs with your children (dates, what you did)
  • Communication log with DCF (dates, who, what was discussed)
  • Photos of your home (if relevant to case)
  • Medical records (yours and children's if relevant)
  • School records (report cards, attendance)
  • Receipts for child-related expenses you've paid

📱 Contact Information

  • Your attorney's phone number
  • Your case manager's phone number
  • Court address and phone number
  • Emergency contact (someone to call if needed)
  • Relative/placement contacts if children with family
  • Service providers' contact info (therapist, etc.)

💡 Pro Tip: Create a Court Binder

Get a 3-ring binder with dividers and organize everything. Label sections clearly: Court Orders, Case Plan, Service Certificates, Visit Logs, Communication Logs, Medical Records, etc. Bring this to EVERY hearing. Your attorney will thank you.

👔 How to Dress for Court

First impressions matter. Dress like you're going to a job interview, not a casual outing.

✅ DO Wear

  • Business casual or business attire
  • Clean, pressed clothing
  • Closed-toe shoes
  • Minimal jewelry
  • Neutral colors (black, navy, gray, white)
  • Conservative clothing that covers you

Men: Dress pants or khakis, button-down shirt, tie optional but recommended, dress shoes

Women: Dress pants or skirt (knee-length), blouse or conservative dress, closed-toe shoes, minimal makeup

❌ DO NOT Wear

  • Shorts, tank tops, or revealing clothing
  • T-shirts with logos, slogans, or graphics
  • Ripped or torn jeans
  • Flip-flops, sandals, or sneakers
  • Hats or sunglasses indoors
  • Heavy perfume/cologne
  • Excessive jewelry or piercings
  • Visible tattoos if possible (cover them)

Why this matters: Fair or not, judges form opinions based on appearance. Looking professional shows you take this seriously and respect the court. It signals you're stable and responsible. Don't give the judge any reason to doubt you.

⚖️ Courtroom Behavior & Etiquette

"Your Honor" - Always.

  • Stand when the judge enters and exits the courtroom
  • Stand when speaking to the judge
  • Never interrupt the judge
  • Say "Yes, Your Honor" or "No, Your Honor"
  • Never say "yeah," "uh-huh," or nod
  • Speak clearly and audibly

Do NOT interrupt. Write it down and tell your attorney.

  • Bring a notepad and pen to court
  • When DCF or opposing counsel says something false, write it down
  • Pass the note to your attorney (or whisper quietly)
  • Let your attorney address it
  • NEVER argue with DCF directly in court
  • NEVER make outbursts or emotional reactions

Why: Judges hate drama. Stay calm and composed. Let your attorney fight for you - that's their job.

Answer honestly, directly, and briefly.

Rules for answering:

  • Stand up when answering
  • Look at the judge, not your attorney or DCF
  • Answer the specific question asked - don't ramble
  • If you don't understand, say: "I'm sorry Your Honor, I don't understand. Could you please repeat that?"
  • If you don't know, say: "I don't know, Your Honor"
  • NEVER lie - it will destroy your credibility

Example good answers:

Judge: "Have you completed the parenting class?"
You: "Yes, Your Honor. I completed it last week." (Show certificate if you have it)

Judge: "Why did you miss the drug test on March 15th?"
You: "Your Honor, I had to work and couldn't get off. I went the next day and have been compliant since."

Example bad answers:

Judge: "Have you completed the parenting class?"
You: "Well, Your Honor, I tried to sign up but they had a waitlist and then I had car trouble and..."

❌ Too much explanation. Just answer the question. If it needs context, your attorney will provide it.

Who can come:

  • Family members (your parents, siblings)
  • Friends
  • Clergy or spiritual advisors
  • Advocates

Court rules:

  • Most dependency hearings are CLOSED to the public
  • The judge must allow your support person in
  • They can sit in the gallery (back of courtroom)
  • They CANNOT speak or participate
  • They must follow all courtroom rules
  • Tell them dress code and behavior expectations

Who should NOT come:

  • Anyone involved in the allegations
  • Anyone with pending criminal charges
  • Anyone who might cause disruption
  • Current romantic partners (unless relevant to case)

Pro tip: Having one calm, supportive person in court can help you stay grounded. But don't bring a crowd - it can look bad. One or two maximum.

It's okay to show emotion, but try to maintain composure.

  • Bring tissues in your pocket/purse
  • If you need a moment, ask: "Your Honor, may I have a moment to compose myself?"
  • Take deep breaths
  • It's okay to cry - this is emotional - but don't have outbursts
  • Never yell, curse, or make threats
  • If you need a break, your attorney can request one

What judges understand: This is the most stressful situation of your life. Showing you care about your children is not a weakness. But you need to show you can handle stress and control yourself.

Red flags for judges: Yelling, cursing, threatening DCF workers, storming out, throwing things, or violent outbursts. These will hurt your case severely. If you have anger issues, this proves their point. Stay calm no matter what.

📅 The Day Before Court

  • Confirm hearing time and location - Call the clerk if unsure
  • Plan your route and parking - Know where to park, how much it costs
  • Organize all documents - Everything in your binder, labeled
  • Call your attorney - Confirm they'll be there, discuss strategy
  • Prepare your outfit - Try it on, make sure it's clean and pressed
  • Arrange childcare - Make sure someone can watch your children
  • Arrange transportation - Reliable ride, plan to arrive 30 min early
  • Get good sleep - You need to be alert and focused
  • Eat breakfast - Hearings can be long, don't go on empty stomach
  • Review your case plan - Know what you've completed, what's pending
  • Review the issues - Know what DCF is alleging and your responses
  • Prepare mentally - Stay calm, stay focused, you can do this

    • 🌅 Morning of Court

    ✅ Final Checklist

    • Ate breakfast
    • Dressed appropriately
    • Have all documents in binder
    • Have photo ID
    • Phone is charged (to call attorney if needed)
    • Have parking money/change
    • Know exact courtroom number
    • Leaving early enough to arrive 30 min before

    🚗 Before You Leave

    • Double-check you have everything
    • Use the bathroom at home
    • Take any necessary medication
    • Don't smoke right before (smell lingers)
    • Brush teeth, look presentable
    • Turn off stove, lock doors
    • Bring water bottle (some courts allow it)
    • Bring snack in car (hearings can run long)

    🏛️ At the Courthouse

    • Arrive 30 minutes early
    • Go through security (leave prohibited items in car)
    • Find your courtroom
    • Check in with clerk if required
    • Find your attorney, review before hearing
    • Use bathroom before hearing starts
    • Turn phone completely OFF
    • Sit quietly until called

    ⏱️ During the Hearing

    • Stand when judge enters
    • Sit at the table with your attorney
    • Stay calm and quiet
    • Listen carefully to everything
    • Take notes, pass to attorney
    • Answer questions honestly and briefly
    • Show respect to everyone
    • Don't react to things you disagree with

    🚫 What NOT to Do in Court

    These will absolutely hurt your case:

    • ❌ Being late or not showing up
    • ❌ Arguing with DCF workers
    • ❌ Interrupting the judge or attorneys
    • ❌ Lying or exaggerating
    • ❌ Making excuses instead of taking accountability
    • ❌ Blaming others for everything
    • ❌ Getting emotional/angry/loud
    • ❌ Cursing or using inappropriate language
    • ❌ Chewing gum or eating in court
    • ❌ Using your phone (even to check time)
    • ❌ Talking while court is in session
    • ❌ Rolling your eyes or making faces
    • ❌ Bringing children to court
    • ❌ Smelling like smoke, alcohol, or weed
    • ❌ Making threats of any kind
    • ❌ Storming out of the courtroom

    Remember: The judge is watching everything - how you dress, how you act, how you react. They're assessing whether you're stable, responsible, and capable of parenting. Every moment in that courtroom is an audition. Act like the parent you want them to see.

    ✅ After Court

    • Get copies of any orders from the clerk before you leave
    • Talk to your attorney about what happened and next steps
    • Ask questions if you didn't understand something
    • Write down what the judge ordered you to do
    • Update your case plan if tasks changed
    • Schedule next hearing in your calendar immediately
    • Start on new tasks right away - don't wait
    • Document everything from the hearing in your notes
    • Follow up with DCF if ordered to do something specific
    • Thank your attorney - they're fighting for you

    Most Important: Whatever the judge ordered you to do, start immediately. Don't wait. Show at the next hearing that you took the court's orders seriously and acted right away. This builds credibility and shows you're committed to getting your children back.
